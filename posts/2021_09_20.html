Derivatives and the Present

Andrew Kerr - September 20, 2021

Recently I was asked what I would talk about if I had to give a TEDTalk. Here's what I said:

"It would probably be something about how the concepts of a derivative and the present are related. In mathematics, derivatives describe the instantaneous rate of change of a quantity. This is an incredibly paradoxical concept. Rate of change usually refers to variation over a finite time interval, so what does it mean to say that something is changing at a certain rate at a particular instant?

Talking about the present introduces a similar paradox. “Now” is a point in time that has no length. Imagine looking at your watch to tell someone the exact time: by the time you finish saying “it’s —“, a few seconds has passed. Your watch time doesn’t match up with what you said anymore. In order for the concept of the present to make sense, it must be stretched to include the few seconds of the past that live in your short term memory."

I owe most of the ideas about derivatives to my university Calculus curriculum, and about the present to Candace Brower's article, which refers to William James' 1890 Principles of Psychology.
