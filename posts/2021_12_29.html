Typing and Sight Reading

Andrew Kerr - December 29, 2021

I was working on a typing practise exercise the other day, and noticed some similarities between typing fast and sight reading sheet music.

Both activities, done well, require the participant to look well ahead with their eyes to process incoming information as their hands take care of the musical notes or words that are presently being played or typed. This requires a kind of uncoupling hands and eyes, the result of which sometimes feels like a widening of my field of vision in order to percieve larger pieces of musical or textual information. When I am sight reading or typing my most effectively, I have the feeling that I am almost watching myself type or play as I am scanning the page, my primary focus.

To that end, I am curious whether practise in either typing or sight reading can affect one's performance in the other. A useful sight reading exercise that facilitates the eyes and hands moving independently, is to look ahead to the next note on the staff and say its name as you play the previous note, continuing through an entire passage in this way so that you are always looking and one note in front of yourself. As this becomes more comfortable, widening the gap between the played and spoken notes adds additional challenge.

As an experiment, I tried the same exercise with the typing practise I activity, and was surprised to find that it felt much the same as it did as a sight reading exercise. With pracitse, I have noticed a marked increase in my words-per-minute and accuracy as a result of becoming comfortable with looking a few words ahead.

I am looking forward to further exploring the connection between typing and sight reading.
